About NAGPUR

Origin of Nagpur

The city was founded in the early 18th century by Bakht Buland, a Gond raja. It became the capital of the Bhonsles of the Maratha confederacy but in 1817 came under British influence. In 1853 the city lapsed into British control and in 1861 became the capital of the Central Provinces. The advent of the Great Indian Peninsula Railway in 1867 spurred its development as a trade centre. After Indian independence, Nagpur was briefly the capital of Madhya Pradesh state, until 1956, when it was included in the newly formed Bombay state, and then in 1960 it became part of what is now Maharashtra state.

Tari Poha is a traditional breakfast dish from Nagpur. It consists of flattened rice (poha) cooked in a spicy and tangy gravy called tari. The tari is made from a mixture of onions, tomatoes, spices, and sometimes peanuts or other vegetables. The dish is typically garnished with fresh coriander leaves, sev (crispy chickpea noodles), and lemon juice for an added burst of flavor. Tari Poha is known for its unique taste and is a popular street food in Nagpur , enjoyed by locals and visitors alike.

Futala Lake is a prominent tourist destination located in Nagpur.It is an artificial lake constructed during the British era. The lake is renowned for its serene surroundings, recreational activities, and vibrant atmosphere. Futala Lake offers visitors opportunities for boating, with paddle boats and rowing boats available for hire. The lakefront is adorned with colorful lights, making it a popular spot for evening strolls and relaxation. Several food stalls and vendors selling snacks and beverages are also present around the lake, adding to its charm. Overall, Futala Lake is a cherished landmark in Nagpur, providing residents and tourists alike with a refreshing escape from the hustle and bustle of city life.

Diksha Bhoomi is a significant Buddhist monument located in Nagpur.It holds historical and cultural importance as it is the place where Dr. B.R. Ambedkar, a prominent Indian jurist, social reformer, and the architect of the Indian Constitution, converted to Buddhism along with thousands of his followers on October 14, 1956. The term "Diksha Bhoomi" translates to "Land of Initiation" and symbolizes the act of renunciation and conversion to Buddhism by Dr. Ambedkar and his followers. It is considered a pilgrimage site for followers of Buddhism, especially those belonging to the Dalit community, who see Dr. Ambedkar as their leader and seek inspiration from his teachings and actions.
